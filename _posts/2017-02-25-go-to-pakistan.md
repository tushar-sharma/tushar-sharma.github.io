India is a place of myriad colors and buried in those colors, are billion voices. From the slums of Dharavi to the opulent Antilla, India is a kaleidoscope of billion stories paradoxically coexiting each day. So it is only natural that democracy has its roots in India. Though democracy suffered  brief stroke of paralysis during emergency, it re-emerged each time, albeit sluggishly.

In a land of conclicting ideas & cultures, it doesn't behoves CBFC, a statutory censorship and classification body, to censor movies according to its whim. Recent victim of CBFC was "Lipstick Under My Burkha". The official respons from CBFC was that it is a “lady oriented” story that puts “fantasy above life” and contains “sexual scenes, abusive words, audio pornography and a bit sensitive touch about one particular section of society.”

<p>
<img src="https://i.ytimg.com/vi/EpHqeHF8NM0/maxresdefault.jpg" >
</p>

I had a brief conversation with a friend of mine on this censorship. He argued, "Such movies had a bad influence in Indian society. It is a vicious conspiracy to insult the Burkha, an attire worn by women of a particular faith." I conveyed to him that the movie had garnered Spirit of Asia Prize & Oxfam Award for Best Film on Gender Equality, he shrugged off as "corrupt western society bribery to demean Indian culture".

This conversation left me bewildered as I always expected liberal views from educated people. But it dawned to me the such notions are quite prevalent in Indian subcontinent. We often view western society with "loose morals" & place our society in high pedastal of morality. This leds to even educated people defensive to any criticism of their culture.Such mentality has been further fueled by advent of high decibel India's new media.

It is difficult, even for educated people, to grasp the idea of "marital rape". "How can a husband rape his wife?", once a guy quipped, quietly supressing his laugh for such a lunatic question. More than hundred liberal developed countries have criminalized marital rape. Only 36 countries still carries the baggage of depriving women autonomy to their bodies. India pleasantly shares this wonderful club with countries like Saudi Arabia, Pakistan, Nigeria, etc. Even a small Himalayan country, Nepal, has made "marital rape" illegal. Bravo Nepal!

This is quite reminiscent of wemen's suffrage movement. Before nineteen century in many parts around the world, women were denied right to vote. Had I walked back in time four hundred years, and asked a man if a women should have the right to vote? He might have chuckled at such a lunatic question.

I am eager to watch "Lipstick Under my Burkha". Female oriented movies are rarity not only in India but also across the world. An interesting test to guage about gender portrayal is Bechdel test. It asks whether a work of fiction features at least two women or girls who talk to each other about something other than a man or boy. It seems most of the movies fails this test.

Under the garb of certification, CBFC is involved in moral policiing & thus curbing the freedom of expression. Some movies provide mass entertainment for the masses, while reflects uncomfortable aspects of society. Pahlaj Nihalani, hardly object to the beautiful cinematography of movies like "Befikre" which bagged "U/A" certificate despite having so many kissing scenes. While a beautiful movie "Aligarh" receives "A" certificate because it as based on true story of Dr Shrinivas Ramchandra Siras, who was discriminately fired from Aligrah Muslim University for homosexuality. Is the mere dipiction of homosexuality more offensive to Indian masses than kisses & item songs?

Salman Rusdie once narrated an intersting story that is dear to me. In his words, "I were working on the film of Midnight's Children, one of the things that we often discussed was a text dear to our hearts, the Natya Shastra. In the Natya Shastra we see the Gods being a little bit bored in heaven and deciding they wanted entertainment. And so a play was made, about the war between Indra and the Asuras, telling how Indra used his mighty weapons to defeat the demons. When the play was performed for the Gods, the demons were offended by their portrayal. The demons felt that the work insulted them as demons. That demoness was improperly criticized. And they attacked the actors; whereupon Indra and Brahma came to the actors' defence. Gods were positioned at all four corners of the stage, and Indra declared that the stage would be a space where everything could be said and nothing could be prohibited.

So in one of the most ancient of Indian texts we find as explicit and extreme a defence of freedom of expression as you can find anywhere in the world. This is not alien to India. This is our culture, our history and our tradition which we are in danger of forgetting and we would do well to remember it."

Arundhati Roy once said Kashmir needs Azaadi (freedom) from India. Dozens of right wing activist attacked her house over her comments.  I myself doesn't agree with her sentiments. Yet such violent action as a sign of disagreement worries me.

Aamir Khan, a popular Bollywood actor, had recently experienced an angry mob backlash. In an interview he was asked, is India becoming intolerant? Like a free citizen of India, he merely exercised his fundamental right to express his opinion. He was quoted as saying that his wife was "alarmed" by a number of incidences and suggested that they should probably leave the country. This insensed many people who asked for his boycott. Some parties like Shiv Sena had the audacity to ask him to "go to Pakistan" (an insunation to ridicule in India).<sup><a href='#fn:1' rel='footnote'>1</a></sup>.

India's favourite neighbour, Pakistan, has made news recently. It's high court  had deemed Valentine day unislamic & barred it from public display<sup><a href='#fn:2' rel='footnote'>2</a></sup>. Such exciting ideas have inspired many in India like Bajarang Dal, who find it as national duty to harrass couples & celebrate black day on Valentine's Day.

Aamir Khan has been named 100 most influencial person in the world. His movies are commercial successes — Lagaan was nominated for an Academy Award — but they also display a sense of social responsibility: they tackle important themes, like poverty and education. His TV show, Satyamev Jayate, is part journalism and part talk show, and it confronts India’s deepest social ills, from sexual abuse to caste discrimination.

Asking Aamir Khan or Arundhati Roy to go to Pakistan is not only deriding but also a weakness of some to tolerate unpopular opinions. If I may commit anti-national activity, I suggest Pehlaj Nihani & Bhajrang Dal to consider going to Pakistan for better carrer opportunities. Such bigotry & partriarchy should have no place in a liberal democratic secular India. Sir go to Pakistan, wahan bahot scope hain.
